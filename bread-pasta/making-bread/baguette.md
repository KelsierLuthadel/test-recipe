# Baguette Shaping (Classic Cylindrical Roll Method)

![Baguette Step 1](resources/baguette-1.jpg)

*The baguette shape is perhaps the most iconic French bread form: a long, thin cylinder with tapered ends and characteristic diagonal slashes across the top. The traditional method uses envelope-folding (important for gluten development) followed by careful rolling to create the long, even cylinder. The result is a bread of elegant proportions and distinctive crust pattern.*

**Rising Time:** 45-60 minutes (final proof after shaping)
**Baking Time:** 15-20 minutes (depending on baguette length and oven)
**Yield:** 1-2 baguettes depending on desired size

## Overview
The baguette shape requires technique, but is highly teachable. The key is the envelope-fold step (important for creating layered gluten structure), then the controlled rolling that creates even diameter throughout, followed by careful scoring that guides oven spring. The baguette's thinness means rapid baking; proper oven temperature and placement are critical. The scoring creates the characteristic \"ears\" (crispy flaps along cut lines) that define a well-executed baguette.

## Equipment
- Work surface (lightly floured)
- Rolling hands (no pin needed for this method)
- Baguette tray or canvas banneton, or flat baking sheet with parchment
- Sharp knife or bread lame (for scoring)
- Damp tea towel (for final proof cover)

## Method

### Stage 1 – Flatten Dough
1. After bulk fermentation and degassing, place your baguette dough on a lightly floured work surface.
1. Using the palms of your hands or a rolling pin, gently flatten the dough into a rough rectangular shape.
1. The rectangle should be approximately 12 inches (30 cm) long x 6-8 inches (15-20 cm) wide.
1. The thickness should be approximately 1 inch (2.5 cm).
1. Work gently; you're not trying to fully flatten, just create a manageable shape for folding.

### Stage 2 – Fold into Thirds
1. While grasping the dough along its longest dimension (the 30 cm length), fold the dough into thirds like you would fold a letter to fit into an envelope.
1. **First fold:** Fold one long side (left or right) toward the center, overlapping by approximately 3-4 cm.
1. **Second fold:** Fold the opposite long side toward the center, overlapping the first fold by approximately 3-4 cm, creating a triple-layer long rectangle.
1. Each layer should overlap evenly; this creates layered gluten structure critical to baguette character.
1. Press gently to seal the edges.

### Stage 3 – Create Cavity
1. Using the heel of your hand or your fingers, push into the center of the folded dough along its entire length.
1. Create a long, shallow cavity (approximately 0.5 cm deep) running from one end to the other.
1. This cavity is the sealing channel for the next step.

### Stage 4 – Seal the Seam
1. Bring the front half of the dough (the top layer) up and over the cavity, folding it onto the back half.
1. The two halves should align; the back edge of the top half should meet the top edge of the back half, creating a seam line along what will become the bottom of the baguette.
1. Using the heel of your hand, press firmly along the entire seam to seal it thoroughly.
1. The seam should be airtight; press multiple times along its length to ensure complete sealing.

### Stage 5 – Roll to Length
1. Place the sealed dough with the seam-side down on the work surface.
1. Using your hands, begin rolling the dough back and forth gently.
1. **Start in the center** and apply gentle, even pressure.
1. Gradually move your hands outward toward the ends while continuing to roll, gently elongating the dough to your desired length (typically 24-30 inches/60-75 cm for a French baguette).
1. **Maintain consistent diameter throughout.** Avoid tapering in the middle or creating thick ends; the cylinder should be uniform.
1. Roll gently; don't pull the dough aggressively or you'll create stress and uneven baking.
1. The final diameter should be approximately 2-3 inches (5-7.5 cm).

### Stage 6 – Smooth Ends
1. Once you've achieved the desired length and consistency, gently smooth and taper the ends.
1. The slight taper (not sharp points) at each end is characteristic of French baguettes.
1. Smooth any rough spots or irregularities by gently rolling and pressing.

### Stage 7 – Position on Baguette Tray
1. Transfer the shaped baguette carefully to a baguette tray (a canvas banneton with grooves for baguettes) OR a flat baking sheet lined with parchment paper.
1. If using a baguette tray, place the baguette seam-side up in the groove (the tray will support it during rising).
1. If using a flat sheet, place seam-side down.
1. The baguette should lie flat and straight, not tilted or twisted.
1. Leave adequate space between baguettes (approximately 3-4 inches/8-10 cm) for expansion.

### Stage 8 – Final Rise
1. Cover loosely with a damp tea towel.
1. Place in a warm location (approximately 20-25°C) for 45-60 minutes.
1. The baguette will gradually puff and expand.
1. Gently poke the dough; it should spring back slowly over 2-3 seconds (properly proofed).

### Stage 9 – Score Diagonal Slashes
1. Preheat the oven to 220-240°C (hot oven for baguettes).
1. If using a baguette tray, carefully flip the baguette onto a flat surface or baking sheet seam-side down.
1. Using an extremely sharp knife or bread lame, score the top of the baguette with diagonal slashes.
1. Space the slashes approximately 2-3 inches (5-7.5 cm) apart along the entire length.
1. Typically, 6-8 slashes total along a standard baguette creates the traditional pattern.
1. Cut at approximately 45-degree angle to the baguette's length.
1. Score depth should be approximately 0.5 cm, deep enough to guide expansion, not so deep as to penetrate fully through.
1. The slashes will open dramatically during baking, creating the characteristic \"ears.\"

### Stage 10 – Bake with Steam
1. Place the scored baguette in the preheated very-hot oven on the middle rack.
1. If possible, create steam in the oven (place a pan of boiling water on the bottom rack, or spray the oven walls with water immediately after placing the baguette).
1. Steam is critical for baguettes, it allows maximum oven spring before the crust sets.
1. Bake for 15-20 minutes until deeply golden brown.
1. The slashes should have opened dramatically, creating crispy \"ears.\"
1. The crust should be crackling and crispy.

### Stage 11 – Cool
1. Remove from the oven using oven gloves and a spatula.
1. Transfer to a wire cooling rack.
1. Cool for at least 30 minutes (preferably 1 hour) before slicing.
1. Baguettes continue to cook internally as they cool; early cutting creates a gummy crumb.
1. The crust will continue to crisp as it cools.

## Notes
- **Envelope-Fold Critical:** This step develops important layered gluten structure; don't skip it or rush it. Proper overlapping ensures even baking.
- **Seam Sealing:** The seam must be thoroughly sealed; inadequate sealing causes the baguette to \"burst\" unevenly on the sides during baking rather than along the scored lines.
- **Consistent Diameter:** Uniform rolling ensures even baking; thick spots cook unevenly and create irregular crumb structure.
- **Scoring Technique:** Confident, swift scoring creates clean cuts with defined \"ears.\" Hesitant scoring creates ragged, uncontrolled expansion.
- **Oven Temperature:** Baguettes require a very hot oven (220-240°C); moderate temperature creates pale, soft crust rather than crispy, golden crust.
- **Steam Critical:** Steam is essential for maximum oven spring and crust formation. Without steam, baguettes bake dry and crust sets before full expansion.
- **Taper vs. Points:** Gentle taper at the ends is characteristic; sharp points burn and look amateurish.

## Variations
- **Multiple Smaller Baguettes:** Divide dough into 2-3 portions and create smaller baguettes; reduces baking time to 12-15 minutes
- **Whole Wheat:** Use 20-30% whole wheat flour for nuttier flavor and darker crust
- **Herb Additions:** Roll fresh herbs into dough during envelope-fold step for flavored baguettes
- **Longer Fermentation:** Allow first fermentation to extend 2-4 hours (instead of standard ~2 hours) for more sour, complex flavor
- **Seeded:** Brush very lightly with water and roll in sesame, poppy, or sunflower seeds before final rise

## Serving
Serve: Fresh, warm from the oven
Best consumed: Within a few hours of baking; day-old baguettes are stale
Usage: Perfect for sandwiches, dipping in soups, spreading with butter or jam
Temperature: Serve warm; baguettes are best eaten within 2-3 hours

## Storage
- Best consumed within 4-6 hours of baking
- Store at room temperature in a paper bag for 1 day maximum (plastic bag makes crust soggy)
- After 1 day, slice and freeze for up to 1 month
- Refresh stale baguette: Sprinkle lightly with water and warm at 180°C for 3-5 minutes
- Do not refrigerate; cold stales bread rapidly
